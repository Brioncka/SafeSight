Why Safesight?

The problem is that many people lose their lives due to unsuspected deer and other wildlife collisions while operating their motor vehicle. My motivation behind this project is to preserve human life and create an opportunity for drivers to safely travel and detect wildlife on the roadway and avoid colliding with it.

Safesight explores how a computer-vision model (AlexNet) can detect wildlife on a driver’s route using dashcam-style images, laying the groundwork for future in-car safety systems.

Project at a Glance

Dataset

Safesight uses a small but focused custom image dataset of dashcam-style scenes. I collected around 320 images from Google Images across two classes: deer and nodeer. After cleaning and loading, the final dataset used for training contains 192 training images and 115 validation images (roughly a 60/40 split), organized into train/deer, train/nodeer, valid/deer, and valid/nodeer.

Model

The core model is a pretrained AlexNet adapted for binary classification (deer vs nodeer) by replacing the final classifier layer with two output units. I use transfer learning from ImageNet so the network can reuse strong visual features and fine-tune them on wildlife detection. For comparison, I also trained an untrained AlexNet (random initialization) and a deeper ResNet18 model on the same dataset.

Performance

Experiments were tracked with Weights & Biases (W&B) to compare batch sizes, learning rates, data augmentation, and architectures:

  • Best pretrained AlexNet: validation accuracy up to 0.9826 (≈ 98.3%) with batch size 16, learning rate 0.0001, and augmentation.
  • Typical strong AlexNet runs: validation accuracy in the 0.96–0.98 range with batch sizes 32 and 64 at learning rate 0.0001.
  • Untrained AlexNet: training from scratch on the same data reached only about 0.80 (80%) validation accuracy, showing the benefit of transfer learning.
  • ResNet18 (pretrained): achieved 1.00 (100%) validation accuracy with very low validation loss, slightly outperforming AlexNet on this task.

Overall, the results suggest that pretrained convolutional networks, especially ResNet18, can reliably detect deer vs nodeer even with a relatively small, real-world style dataset.

Training Dynamics

Loss Curve

Training vs. validation loss over 30 epochs. Pretrained runs quickly drive loss down and stabilize at a low validation loss (around 0.10–0.20), while untrained runs remain noticeably higher and more unstable.

Accuracy Curve

Training vs. validation accuracy over 30 epochs. Pretrained AlexNet climbs to high validation accuracy (≈ 97–99%) within a few epochs and stays there, whereas untrained AlexNet improves more slowly and plateaus around 85–90%.

What I Learned & What’s Next

Key Takeaways

  • Transfer learning works extremely well: a pretrained AlexNet dramatically outperforms an untrained AlexNet on a small, custom dataset.
  • Data quality matters: having clear, on-topic images for each class is crucial for reliable wildlife detection.
  • Experiment tracking helps: using Weights & Biases made it easy to compare dozens of runs and clearly see the gap between pretrained and untrained models.

Future Work

  • Collect more diverse, real dashcam footage (nighttime, rain, fog) to better reflect real-world driving scenarios.
  • Extend beyond binary deer/nodeer to multi-class wildlife detection (deer, elk, moose, other animals, no animal).
  • Prototype an in-car or mobile app that could run a lightweight version of the model in real time as a driver assistance tool.
